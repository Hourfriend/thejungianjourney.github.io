I
Phase One

Meeting the Shadow

Jung called the Shadow the dark side of the personality — not evil, but unknown. Everything we have disowned, suppressed, or been told was unacceptable lives here. Before integration comes recognition. This week you will learn to see your own shadow clearly, without flinching.

"One does not become enlightened by imagining figures of light, but by making the darkness conscious."— Carl Gustav Jung
1
Day One
What is the Shadow? First encounter
Reflection▾
Today's Practice
Find a quiet space with paper and a pen — no phone, no screens. Write for 15 uninterrupted minutes on this question: "What parts of myself do I never want anyone to see?" Write without editing. Let the uncomfortable truths land on the page. This is your first descent. Nothing you write here is wrong. The Shadow only has power in the dark.
Jungian ContextThe Shadow forms in childhood. Every time we were told "don't be angry," "don't be selfish," "don't be so much" — those parts of us didn't disappear. They went underground. The Shadow is not your enemy. It is the part of you still waiting to come home.
2
Day Two
The mirror of projection
Journal▾
Today's Practice
Think of the person who irritates, repels, or angers you most right now. Write their name. Then answer these prompts with brutal honesty — the discomfort is the point:
  • ◈What specific quality in them makes you react so strongly?
  • ◈Is there any part of you that carries this same quality — even secretly, even occasionally?
  • ◈What would it mean about you if you admitted you shared this trait?
  • ◈What do you wish you could say to them — and why does it feel dangerous to say it?
Jungian ContextJung taught that we project our Shadow onto others — the qualities we cannot accept in ourselves become the qualities we despise in the world. The person who triggers you most is often holding a mirror to your own disowned self.
3
Day Three
Childhood wounding — where the shadow began
Journal▾
Today's Practice
Return to early memory. Write about a time before age 12 when you were shamed, punished, or rejected for expressing a part of yourself. Answer with gentleness:
  • ◈What part of you were you told — explicitly or implicitly — to hide?
  • ◈What did you decide about yourself in that moment?
  • ◈How does that decision still govern you today?
  • ◈Write a sentence to the child version of yourself who made that decision.
Jungian ContextThe Shadow is not chosen — it is built from the raw material of early experience. What was rejected by the family or society became rejected by the self. Shadow work begins with understanding this is not weakness. It was survival.
4
Day Four
Active imagination — dialogue with the Shadow
Ritual▾
Today's Practice
This is Jung's most powerful technique: Active Imagination. Sit in a dim, quiet space. Close your eyes and visualise your Shadow as a figure. Let it emerge without forcing it. Then write a dialogue between yourself and this figure. Ask it: "What do you need from me? What have I denied you? What are you trying to tell me?" Write whatever comes, even if it disturbs you.
Jungian ContextActive Imagination was Jung's own primary method of engaging the unconscious. He filled entire notebooks — the Red Book — with dialogues like this. The Shadow figure, when given a voice, almost always reveals something it needs rather than something threatening.
5
Day Five
The golden shadow — disowned gifts
Reflection▾
Today's Practice
Not all of the Shadow is darkness. The Golden Shadow holds the gifts, talents, and greatness we were told was arrogant, dangerous, or too much. Write about:
  • ◈Who do you deeply admire? What quality in them makes you feel something — awe, envy, longing?
  • ◈Was there a dream for yourself you buried because it felt too big or selfish?
  • ◈What would you do if no one could judge you and failure was impossible?
  • ◈What compliment is hardest for you to receive — and why?
Jungian ContextJung wrote that we project not only our darkness but our unlived greatness onto others — we idolise in them what we have forbidden in ourselves. Reclaiming the Golden Shadow is as transformative as integrating the dark.
6
Day Six
Dream incubation — asking the unconscious
Ritual▾
Tonight's Practice
Before sleep tonight: place a notebook beside your bed. Write one clear question to your unconscious. Read it aloud once before sleep. When you wake, do not move, do not check your phone. Lie still and recall any images, figures, feelings from your dreams. Write them immediately. Then reflect: what might any symbol represent?
Jungian ContextFor Jung, the dream was not a puzzle to be solved but a message to be received. The figures in dreams are often aspects of the dreamer's own psyche — the Shadow, the Anima, the Wise Old Man.
7
Day Seven
First integration — what have you seen?
Review▾
Today's Practice
Read back through everything you have written this week. Then write your honest answers to:
  • ◈What surprised you most about what you discovered?
  • ◈Which day's work felt most threatening — and what does that tell you?
  • ◈What part of your Shadow have you already begun to accept?
  • ◈Write one sentence of compassion to the part of you that spent years hiding.
Jungian ContextIntegration is not a moment of dramatic revelation. It is the slow, repeated act of choosing to see — and choosing not to look away. You have completed the first descent. The next phase goes deeper.
II
Phase Two

Anima & Animus

Beyond the Shadow lies the Anima (the feminine within the male psyche) and the Animus (the masculine within the female). These are not about gender — they are about the qualities we have suppressed because they were assigned to the opposite sex. Undeveloped, they wreak havoc in our relationships. Integrated, they become a source of extraordinary creative and relational power.

"The anima is the archetype of life itself."— Carl Gustav Jung
8
Day Eight
Mapping your inner opposite
Reflection▾
Today's Practice
Draw two columns. Label one "Qualities I was encouraged to embody" and the other "Qualities I was told were not for me." Fill both honestly. Pay attention to gendered messages. The second column is where your Anima or Animus lives. Write about how life might be different if you had full access to those suppressed qualities right now.
Jungian ContextThe Anima/Animus is not about becoming more feminine or masculine. It is about becoming whole. The suppressed inner opposite is the source of some of our deepest moods, most irrational reactions, and most intense attractions.
9
Day Nine
Romantic projection — who are you seeing?
Journal▾
Today's Practice
Think of a person you have been intensely attracted to, infatuated with, or obsessed by. Write in detail about what drew you. Then answer:
  • ◈What specific qualities did you see in them that felt almost magnetic?
  • ◈Do you carry any of these qualities yourself? Why or why not?
  • ◈When the relationship or fantasy ended — what exactly was shattered?
  • ◈What were you really seeking when you sought this person?
Jungian ContextFalling in love is, in large part, recognising your own Anima or Animus in another person. When the projection lifts and the real person appears, many relationships collapse. What felt like love may have been a call to integrate something in yourself.
10
Day Ten
The four stages — where is your inner figure?
Reflection▾
Today's Practice
Jung described four developmental stages of the Anima/Animus. Which stage describes your inner opposite's current development?
  • 1Eve / Tarzan — purely instinctual, biological.
  • 2Helen / Faust — romanticised idealism; the inner opposite projected as a perfect ideal.
  • 3Mary / Bearer of meaning — spiritual and relational; a source of values and depth.
  • 4Sophia / Merlin — wisdom incarnate; fully integrated as a guide to the deepest self.
Jungian ContextMost people oscillate between stages 1 and 2 for much of their lives. The work of stages 3 and 4 is the work of genuine maturity: finding the source of meaning, depth, and wisdom within.
11
Day Eleven
Embodying the opposite — a day's experiment
Active▾
Today's Practice
Choose one quality from your "suppressed" column from Day 8. For the next 24 hours, consciously experiment with expressing this quality. If you suppressed softness — allow one moment of genuine vulnerability. If you suppressed assertiveness — say one thing directly you would normally soften. At the end of the day, write what happened in your body when you tried it.
Jungian ContextIntegration is not intellectual. It requires the body. Acting from the suppressed inner opposite — even briefly — begins to dissolve the belief that these qualities are dangerous or impossible for you.
12
Day Twelve
The inner marriage — a visualisation
Ritual▾
Today's Practice
Find 20 uninterrupted minutes. Close your eyes. Visualise yourself in a vast, ancient hall. A figure walks toward you: your Anima or Animus in their most developed form. Let them approach. Let them speak. Ask: "What have you been trying to give me? What do we need to do together?" Write everything when you return.
Jungian ContextJung called the union of the conscious self with the Anima or Animus the "inner marriage" — a sacred conjunction which was the highest alchemical metaphor for psychological wholeness.
13
Day Thirteen
Moods, possession & the negative complex
Journal▾
Today's Practice
Think of a recent moment when you were "not yourself" — gripped by an inexplicable mood, rage, or despair. Describe the episode, then write:
  • ◈What triggered it? How suddenly did it arrive?
  • ◈What did the mood want you to do or say?
  • ◈What core belief about yourself was being threatened?
  • ◈Whose voice did the inner critic sound like?
Jungian ContextWhen the Anima or Animus is undeveloped, it erupts as moods and compulsions. Jung called this being "possessed" by the archetype. Naming the possession while it happens begins to loosen its grip.
14
Day Fourteen
Week II review — what has shifted in you?
Review▾
Today's Practice
Two weeks of descent. Write freely, spending at least 5 minutes on each:
  • ◈How have your relationships looked different since you began to see projection at work?
  • ◈What quality of your inner opposite do you most want to embody by the end of this journey?
  • ◈What moment this week felt most like genuine inner contact — most real, most alive?
Jungian ContextHalfway through. The unconscious rarely announces its transformations loudly. More often there is a quiet shift — a moment where a familiar trigger no longer hooks you the same way.
III
Phase Three

The Archetypes

Beneath the personal unconscious lies the collective unconscious — a vast inherited ocean of human experience expressed through archetypes. The Persona, the Wise Old Man, the Great Mother, the Trickster, the Hero. These are not metaphors. They are living structures shaping your behaviour, your relationships, and your fate.

"The privilege of a lifetime is to become who you truly are."— Carl Gustav Jung
15
Day Fifteen
The Persona — the mask you wear
Journal▾
Today's Practice
Write about your primary Persona: how do you present at work, in public, with strangers? Then write about the moment the mask slips — when do you feel most unlike the self you perform? What are you most afraid people would discover if the mask came off completely?
Jungian ContextThe Persona is not a lie — it is a social tool. The problem arises when we identify with it so completely that we forget there is someone wearing it. The stronger the Persona, often the more chaotic the Shadow beneath it.
16
Day Sixteen
The Hero's journey — your myth so far
Journal▾
Today's Practice
Write the story of your life as a mythological journey:
  • ◈The ordinary world: what was your starting point — your "before"?
  • ◈The call: what event, loss, or awakening pulled you from that world?
  • ◈The ordeal: what is the darkest challenge you have faced or are facing?
  • ◈The boon: what wisdom or gift has the ordeal given you?
  • ◈The return: what are you being called to bring back — to offer?
Jungian ContextJung saw myth as the psyche's native language. The Hero's journey is not a story template — it is a description of the individuation process itself.
17
Day Seventeen
The Trickster — where chaos enters your life
Reflection▾
Today's Practice
Write about where the Trickster appears in your life:
  • ◈When do you self-sabotage — undermining something you consciously want?
  • ◈Where does chaos seem to follow you despite your best plans?
  • ◈Is there a way in which the disruptions in your life were also, secretly, liberating?
  • ◈What rigid structure in your current life might the Trickster be trying to dissolve?
Jungian ContextLoki, Coyote, Hermes, the court jester. The Trickster appears in every mythology because it serves a universal psychic function: it prevents the psyche from calcifying into rigidity.
18
Day Eighteen
The Wise Elder — meeting your inner guide
Ritual▾
Today's Practice
Return to Active Imagination. Close your eyes and descend to a quiet inner landscape. A figure appears — old, unhurried, radiating warmth you recognise. Let them approach. Ask: "What do I most need to understand about my life right now? What have I been refusing to see?" Write the conversation in full when you return.
Jungian ContextThe Wise Old Man/Woman is the archetype of spirit and meaning. When you feel completely lost, this figure emerges from the psyche as a compensating force, offering perspective the panicking ego cannot generate alone.
19
Day Nineteen
The wounded healer — your deepest wound as gift
Journal▾
Today's Practice
Write about your deepest wound: the thing that has caused you the most suffering, shame, or limitation. Then:
  • ◈What understanding has this wound given you that you could not have gotten any other way?
  • ◈Who have you been able to meet, truly meet, because of it?
  • ◈Is there a way in which this wound has been quietly shaping your purpose?
  • ◈If this wound could speak, what would it ask of you?
Jungian ContextThe archetype of the Wounded Healer holds the paradox that the healer's power comes precisely from their wound. The wound that has been met becomes compassion, wisdom, and genuine capacity to help others.
20
Day Twenty
Synchronicity — the psyche speaking through events
Reflection▾
Today's Practice
Spend today in heightened attention. In the evening, write about:
  • ◈A synchronicity from the past — a "coincidence" that felt too meaningful to dismiss
  • ◈What was happening in your inner life at the time it occurred?
  • ◈What did it seem to be pointing toward?
Jungian ContextJung did not see synchronicity as magic. He saw it as evidence that the psyche and the world are not fully separate systems. When an unusual coincidence arrests you during inner work — the unconscious may be using the outer world as its canvas.
21
Day Twenty-One
Week III review — your personal mythology
Review▾
Today's Practice
Write about which archetype has shown up most powerfully in your life so far — the Hero, the Trickster, the Wounded Healer, the Wise Elder. Then write: what does your personal mythology seem to be about? What is it trying to become?
Jungian ContextThe whole purpose of meeting these figures is to stop being unconsciously lived by them and to begin consciously living with them. One more week. The final work is integration and the first steps toward individuation.
IV
Phase Four

Individuation

Individuation — the process of becoming a unified, whole, undivided Self — was Jung's term for the central purpose of human psychological life. Not perfection. Not the elimination of the Shadow. But the conscious integration of all parts into a living, breathing wholeness.

"The most terrifying thing is to accept oneself completely."— Carl Gustav Jung
22
Day Twenty-Two
The Self — the totality you are becoming
Reflection▾
Today's Practice
Draw or describe your own mandala today: a circular symbol that represents all of you — light and dark, known and unknown, past and potential. It doesn't need to be art. It can be words, colours, shapes. Afterwards write: what does wholeness feel like to you — not as an idea, but as a physical sensation?
Jungian ContextThe mandala — appearing spontaneously in the dreams and art of people undergoing deep inner transformation — was Jung's central symbol for the Self. Your task is not to force wholeness. Your task is to stop obstructing it.
23
Day Twenty-Three
Reclaiming disowned qualities — an act of will
Active▾
Today's Practice
Choose the single quality that would most transform your life if you reclaimed it fully. Write a formal declaration: "I reclaim ________. This quality is mine. I am no longer willing to project it, hide it, or punish it in others." Then write what one concrete action this week would demonstrate this reclamation in the world.
Jungian ContextIntegration is not just a psychological event — it must become behavioural. The reclaimed quality must be expressed, tested, lived. The unconscious respects action more than intention.
24
Day Twenty-Four
The unlived life — what are you postponing?
Journal▾
Today's Practice
Write about your unlived life with unsparing honesty:
  • ◈What version of yourself did you abandon because it felt too risky, selfish, or unlikely?
  • ◈What would you be doing if you were living without the constraints of others' expectations?
  • ◈What would your 80-year-old self most regret not having tried?
  • ◈What is the fear underneath the postponement — and is it still true?
Jungian Context"The unlived life," Jung wrote, "is the greatest source of neurosis." The call to live your life is not indulgence. It is psychological necessity.
25
Day Twenty-Five
Grief — mourning what was never allowed to be
Ritual▾
Today's Practice
Give yourself permission to mourn the unlived life. Write a letter to the version of yourself that was not allowed to exist — the angry child, the ambitious woman, the tender man, the strange one. Tell them what happened. Tell them you are sorry. Tell them what you plan to do now.
Jungian ContextGrief is not weakness in the individuation process — it is evidence of contact with what was real. Mourning the unlived life clears the ground for genuine new growth. You cannot build the future on unacknowledged loss.
26
Day Twenty-Six
Forgiveness as psychological act — not moral duty
Journal▾
Today's Practice
Identify the person or circumstance you most resent. Write everything: what happened, how it hurt, what it took from you. Then write about what holding this resentment has cost you over time. Finally: what would it mean to release it — not for their sake, but as an act of reclaiming your own energy?
Jungian ContextResentment, unexamined, is a form of ongoing psychological possession by the person who hurt you. Jung saw the work of releasing resentment as practical liberation of psychic energy that could return to the living of life.
27
Day Twenty-Seven
Your values beneath the Persona
Reflection▾
Today's Practice
Strip away the Persona entirely in writing. Who are you when no one is watching and nothing is at stake? Write your deepest values — not the ones you perform, but the ones that cause you genuine pain when violated. Where in your current life are you living in alignment with these values? Where are you betraying them — and for what?
Jungian ContextIndividuation requires a ruthless commitment to authenticity over conformity. The fully individuated person is free from the particular suffering of pretending to be something they are not.
28
Day Twenty-Eight
A letter from your future integrated Self
Ritual▾
Today's Practice
Close your eyes. Imagine yourself at the end of a long life — fully individuated, integrated, at peace. From that place, write a letter to yourself today. What does that future self most want you to know? What are you worrying about that will not matter? What are you postponing that you must begin now?
Jungian ContextThe individuated Self exists as a potential — a telos, a direction — that the psyche is always moving toward. What it says to you is not fantasy. It is the deeper knowing of your own psyche speaking without the ego's interference.
29
Day Twenty-Nine
Your shadow work manifesto
Journal▾
Today's Practice
Write your personal manifesto — a declaration of what you have seen, what you are claiming, and what you are done with. Include: the Shadow qualities you are integrating, the unlived life you are beginning to live, the projections you are withdrawing, the values you are committing to, and the one thing you will no longer be willing to do to yourself. Keep this. Return to it when the old patterns resurface.
Jungian ContextThe manifesto is a psychic act as much as a written one. The unconscious does not forget what the conscious mind has clearly declared — even when the ego temporarily forgets, the deeper self remembers.
30
Day Thirty
The return — bringing the gold back up
Completion▾
Today's Practice
You descended. You met what was hidden. Now you return. Write about what you are carrying back up:
  • ◈What did you find in the Shadow that turned out to be a gift?
  • ◈What belief about yourself are you leaving underground — permanently?
  • ◈Who are you now, in this moment, that you were not 30 days ago?
  • ◈What is the first thing you will do differently in your daily life from tomorrow?
Then: go outside. Walk. Feel the ground. You have done something real.
Jungian Context"The gold is in the dark." This is the oldest alchemical truth and Jung's most enduring gift to psychology. Individuation is not a destination — it is a lifelong orientation toward wholeness. Every descent that ends in a conscious return changes the person permanently. You are not who you were. That is everything.
